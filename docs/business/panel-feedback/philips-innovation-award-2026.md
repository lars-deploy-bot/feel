## Critical Action Items

### 1. Nail the ICP (Immediate Priority)

**Problem**: "Startups, scale-ups, and SMBs" is too broad.

**Actions**:
- Pick ONE segment where workflows are repeatable and valuable
- Define specific industries (retail? services? professional services?)
- Document: pain points, trigger events, budget owner, must-have integrations
- Create job-to-be-done statements

### 2. Prove Differentiation with Outcomes (Immediate Priority)

**Problem**: Panel doesn't understand what makes Alive different from Webflow + Zapier/Make.

**Actions**:
- Quantify: "Reduces tools from X to Y", "Saves Z hours/week"
- Document: "Responds to leads in <5 minutes"
- Create comparison tables vs competitors
- Gather customer testimonials with specific metrics

### 3. Build Financial Model (Week 1)

**Problem**: No financials provided. Only reference is "40 EUR/month" which requires massive subscriber base.

**Actions**:
- Unit economics: CAC, LTV, payback period
- Cost structure: infrastructure, support, development
- Revenue model: setup fee + subscription, or tiered by complexity
- Churn expectations
- Time to break-even projections

### 4. Clarify Value Proposition (Week 1-2)

**Problem**: Promise is understood but solution is unclear.

**Current promise**: "Tools to build website + back-end processes in controlled way"

**Missing**: How Alive actually delivers this. Be specific about:
- What "workflow-driven website" means concretely
- How AI makes the difference (not just "AI plays important role")
- What backend automation looks like

### 5. Go-to-Market Strategy (Week 2)

**Problem**: No clear roadmap. Strategy is "target via existing network."

**Actions**:
- Define acquisition channels beyond founder network
- Create customer onboarding process (phone call? digital-only? training?)
- Map path from service-led to self-serve
- Define what becomes templated vs custom

## Panelist-Specific Insights

### Panelist 1 (Harshest Critic)

**Main concern**: No differentiation from competitors.

**Quote**: "I don't see how this idea is different from competitors that also offer automation modules/options."

**Actionable**:
- Research to understand target group needs before product work
- Define USP within startup market specifically
- More market data on Netherlands
- Assess specific needs per segment

### Panelist 2 (Most Constructive)

**Main concern**: Business value not explicit enough.

**Quote**: "What I miss is the real value of the proposition! What is being said in terms of the promise, is not very well described!"

**Strongest points identified**:
- Workflow-driven Website approach (USP)
- Real customer interaction in development phase
- Technical scalability

**Recommendations**:
- Add business analyst/consultant skill to team
- Focus on product development over marketing/sales short-term
- Pick limited industries first for focus
- Make Business Value statement crystal clear

### Panelist 3 (Product-Focused)

**Main concern**: Retention and growth path unclear.

**Questions to answer**:
1. How does customer base evolve as product develops?
2. How do you retain clients as they become more sophisticated?
3. What does onboarding look like for non-digital-native customers?
4. Any "online visibility" features planned (SEO, Google Ads, social)?
5. What analytics can you run to learn from best-performing websites?

**Opportunity**: Scale learnings from successful sites to new clients.

### Panelist 4 (Most Balanced)

**What's working**:
- Clear pain identified (SMBs need lead/booking ops, not just pretty sites)
- Sensible early strategy (hands-on projects to learn and productize)
- Team credibility
- Early traction signals (8 customers)

**Highest leverage improvements**:
1. Nail the wedge + ICP
2. Prove differentiation with outcomes
3. Fix packaging/pricing story
4. Reliability as product principle (monitoring, rollback, testing, failure states)
5. Show path from service-led to self-serve

**Pricing suggestion**: Setup fee + subscription, or tiered by automation complexity/integrations.

### Panelist 5 (Sales-Focused)

**Main concern**: Need sales capability to explain differentiation.

**Quote**: "You need good sales people to get the proposition across and explain the difference vs other SaaS providers."

**Actions**:
- Hire or develop sales capability
- Create sales enablement materials
- Define time & cost savings clearly
- Simplify explanation of value

## Consensus Themes

### Strengths (All Panelists Agree)

1. **Technical scalability** - No doubts about ability to scale the platform
2. **Team capability** - Engineering and product skills are solid
3. **Real customer engagement** - Early hands-on work validates learning approach
4. **Problem is real** - SMBs do struggle with website + backend automation

### Weaknesses (All Panelists Agree)

1. **No financials** - Zero unit economics or projections
2. **Unclear differentiation** - Why Alive vs Webflow + Zapier?
3. **Too broad ICP** - "Startups and SMBs" spans everything
4. **Vague go-to-market** - No clear acquisition or onboarding strategy
5. **Crowded market** - Many competitors, need sharper positioning

### Panelist 3

> Value proposition including the workflow dynamics gives Alive potentially an edge versus peers, but the market is very crowded. On top of this, it's difficult to navigate, particularly for the target customer base. Indeed, very scaleable; Offerings in local language, according to local regulation, should be easily feasible.

>
> Retention: Current target audience is start ups/SME. How would you retain clients as they grow and potentially require more sophisticated / tailored online solutions?
>

How would you look at integrating "online visibility" in the functions of Alive?
>

This feature does sit without the direct remit of Alive as it is, but would take a further step in solving the (perceived) online complexity & visibility for SMEs.

### Panelist 4

> The "AI website builder" space is crowded, but Alive's workflow-first angle (forms/bookings as automations, plus "opinionated setups" and reusable blueprints) is a meaningful twist. The plan doesn't yet prove defensibility vs existing builder + automation stacks.
>
> Clear productivity impact for SMBs (less manual admin; fewer disconnected tools). Not positioned as "life-changing," but valuable if it reliably saves time and reduces tool chaos.
>
> USPs are articulated (integrated automations, editable AI drafts, setup library). However, the plan needs sharper proof: why customers choose Alive over Webflow/Wix + Zapier/Make, or an agency, and what "better" concretely means (time saved, fewer tools, higher conversion, etc.).
>
> The template + automation blueprint library can scale in principle, and the plan acknowledges the early hands-on phase. The big open question: can this truly become self-serve without heavy human implementation/support?
>
> Complementary co-founders (engineering + product/design), relevant experience, and self-awareness of gaps (sales/partnerships/financial planning). Strong early-stage execution potential.
>
> Good qualitative overview and clear competitor landscape (builders, AI builders, agencies). Missing: market sizing, segmentation, and a sharper "wedge" where Alive wins first.
>
> You describe two customer types (early-stage startups and SMBs with outdated sites) and state eight customers—that's strong traction for Rough Diamond. Still, the ICP is broad; the "why they buy" could be more concrete (job-to-be-done, trigger events, budget owner, must-have integrations).
>
> The initial "done-with-you" approach makes sense for learning, but the roadmap to scalable acquisition and self-serve adoption is underdeveloped. Pricing and delivery model also raise feasibility questions (see Financials).
>
> Risks are clearly identified (positioning, agency-to-product transition, automation reliability, dev capacity) with sensible mitigations.
>

>
> **What's working:**
> - Clear pain: SMBs don't just need a pretty site; they need lead/booking ops that actually run.
> - Sensible early strategy: use hands-on projects to learn and productize reusable patterns.
> - Team credibility and early traction signals.
>
> **Main points of attention (highest leverage):**
> 1. Nail the wedge + ICP. "Startups and SMBs" is too broad. Pick one segment where your workflows are repeatable and valuable, and own it.
> 2. Prove differentiation with outcomes. Add concrete comparisons: "Alive reduces tools from X to Y," "saves Z hours/week," "responds to leads in <5 minutes," etc.

> 4. Reliability as a product principle. Workflow breakage kills trust. Build guardrails: monitoring, human-readable flow logic, rollback, testing, and clear failure states.
> 5. Show the path from service-led to self-serve. Define what becomes templated, what remains custom, and when customers can onboard without you.
